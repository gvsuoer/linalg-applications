Recall that we can visualize the action of a matrix transformation defined by a diagonalizable matrix by using a change of basis. For example, let \(T(\vx) = A\vx\text{,}\) where \(A = \frac{1}{3}\left[ \begin{array}{rr} 8\amp -1\\-2\amp 7 \end{array} \right]\text{.}\) The eigenvalues of \(A\) are \(\lambda_1 = 3\) and \(\lambda_2 = 2\) with corresponding eigenvectors \(\vv_1 = [-1 \ 1]^{\tr}\) and \([1 \ 2]^{\tr}\text{.}\) So \(A\) is diagonalizable by the matrix \(P = \left[ \begin{array}{rc} -1\amp 1\\1\amp 2 \end{array} \right]\text{,}\) with \(P^{-1}AP = D = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 3\amp 0\\0\amp 2 \end{array} \right]\text{.}\) Note that \(T(\vx) = PDP^{-1} \vx\text{.}\) Now \(P^{-1}\) is a change of basis matrix from the standard basis to the basis \(\CB = \{\vv_1, \vv_2\}\text{,}\) and \(D\) stretches space in the direction of \(\vv_1\) by a factor of 3 and stretches space in the direction of \(\vv_2\) by a factor of 2, and then \(P\) changes basis back to the standard basis.
