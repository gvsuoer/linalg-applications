Section Exercises

Checkpoint 1.13.

Given vectors \(\vu=\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \end{array} \right]\) and \(\vv=\left[ \begin{array}{r} -1 \\ 2 \end{array} \right]\) in \(\R^2\text{,}\) determine if \(\vw=\left[ \begin{array}{r} -4 \\ -1 \end{array} \right]\) can be written as a linear combination of \(\vu\) and \(\vv\text{.}\) If so, determine the weights of \(\vu\) and \(\vv\) which produce \(\vw\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 1.14.

Given vectors \(\vv_1=\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \\1 \end{array} \right]\text{,}\) \(\vv_2=\left[ \begin{array}{r} -2 \\ 1 \\2 \end{array} \right]\) and \(\vv_3=\left[ \begin{array}{r} -1 \\ 3\\3 \end{array} \right]\) in \(\R^3\text{,}\) determine if \(\vw=\left[ \begin{array}{c} 5\\ 5\\ 1 \end{array} \right]\) can be written as a linear combination of \(\vv_1\text{,}\) \(\vv_2\) and \(\vv_3\text{.}\) If so, determine the weights of \(\vv_1\text{,}\) \(\vv_2\) and \(\vv_3\) which produce \(\vw\text{.}\) Reflect on the result. Is there anything special about the given vectors \(\vv_1\text{,}\) \(\vv_2\) and \(\vv_3\text{?}\)

Checkpoint 1.15.

Let \(\vu=\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \\1 \end{array} \right]\) and \(\vv=\left[ \begin{array}{r} -1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]\) in \(\R^3\text{.}\) Determine which vectors \(\vw= \left[ \begin{array}{c} w_1 \\ w_2 \\ w_3 \end{array} \right]\) in \(\R^3\) can be written as a linear combination of \(\vu\) and \(\vv\text{.}\) Does the set of \(\vw\)'s include the 0 vector? If so, determine which weights in the linear combination produce the 0 vector. If not, explain why not.

Checkpoint 1.16.

Consider vectors \(\vu=\left[ \begin{array}{c} 0\\ 2\\0 \end{array} \right]\) and \(\vv=\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1\\ 1\\1 \end{array} \right]\) in \(\R^3\text{.}\)

(a)

Find four specific linear combinations of the vectors \(\vu\) and \(\vv\text{.}\)

(b)

Explain why the zero vector must be a linear combination of \(\vu\) and \(\vv\text{.}\)

(c)

What kind of geometric shape does the set of all linear combinations of \(\vu\) and \(\vv\) have in \(\R^3\text{?}\)

(d)

Can we obtain any vector in \(\R^3\) as a linear combination of \(\vu\) and \(\vv\text{?}\) Explain.

Checkpoint 1.17.

Suppose we have two different water-benzene-acetic acid solutions, one with 40% water, 50% benzene and 10% acetic acid, the other with 52% water, 42% benzene and 6% acid.

(a)

An experiment we want to conduct requires a solution with 43% water, 48% benzene and 9% acid. Representing each acid solution as a vector, determine if we can we make this new acid solution by mixing the first two solutions, or do we have to run to the chemical solutions market to get the solution we want?

(b)

Using the water-benzene-acetic acid solutions in the previous problem, can we obtain an acid solution which contains 50% water, 43% benzene and 7% acid?

(c)

Determine the relationship between the percentages of water, benzene, and acid in solutions which can be obtained by mixing the two given water-benzene-acetic acid solutions above.

Checkpoint 1.18.

Is the vector \(\vb = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0\\1\\2 \end{array} \right]\) in \(\Span\left\{ \left[ \begin{array}{r} 2\\-1\\0 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{r} -3\\0\\-5 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1\\1\\0 \end{array} \right] \right\}\text{?}\) Justify your answer.

Checkpoint 1.19.

Describe geometrically each of the following sets.

(a)

\(\Span\left\{\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1\\1 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{r} -1\\-1 \end{array} \right]\right\}\) in \(\R^2\)

(b)

\(\Span\left\{\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1\\1\\1 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{r} -1\\-1\\-1 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{c} 2\\0\\1 \end{array} \right]\right\}\) in \(\R^3\)

Checkpoint 1.20.

Consider the linear system

\begin{alignat*}{5} {2}x_1 \amp {}+{} \amp {3}x_2 \amp {}+{} \amp {3}x_3 \amp {} \amp {} \amp {}={} \amp 0\amp {}\\ x_1 \amp {} \amp {} \amp {}+{} \amp {6}x_3 \amp {}+{} \amp {6}x_4 \amp {}={} \amp 0\amp {}\\ {2}x_1 \amp {}+{} \amp {4}x_2 \amp {}+{} \amp {3}x_3 \amp {}-{} \amp {}x_4 \amp {}={} \amp 0\amp {.} \end{alignat*}

(a)

Find the general solution to this system.

(b)

Find two specific vectors \(\vv_1\) and \(\vv_2\) so that the solution set to this system is \(\Span\{\vv_1, \vv_2\}\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 1.21.

Answer the following question as yes or no. Verify your answer. If \(\vu\) and \(\vv\) are vectors in \(\R^n\text{,}\) then \(\vv\) is in \(\Span\{\vu, \vu-\vv\}\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 1.22.

Let \(\vv\text{,}\) \(\vu\text{,}\) and \(\vw\) be vectors in \(\R^n\) and let \(a\) and \(b\) be scalars. Verify TheoremÂ 1.4. That is, show that

(a)

\(\vv + \vu = \vu + \vv\)

(b)

\((\vv + \vu) + \vw = \vv + (\vu + \vw)\)

(c)

The vector \(\vz = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{array} \right]\) has the property that \(\vv + \vz = \vv\text{.}\)

(d)

\((-1)\vv + \vv = \vz\text{.}\)

(e)

\((a+b) \vv = a\vv + b\vv\)

\(1 \vv = \vv\text{.}\)

Checkpoint 1.23.

Label each of the following statements as True or False. Provide justification for your response.

(a)

A vector in \(\R^2\text{,}\) i.e. a two-dimensional vector, is also a vector in \(\R^3\text{.}\)

(b) True/False.

Any vector in \(\R^2\) can be visualized as a vector in \(\R^3\) by adding a 0 as the last coordinate.

(c) True/False.

The zero vector is a scalar multiple of any other vector (of the same size).

(d) True/False.

The zero vector cannot be a linear combination of two non-zero vectors.

(e) True/False.

Given two vectors \(\vu\) and \(\vv\text{,}\) the vector \(\frac{1}{2}\vu\) is a linear combination of \(\vu\) and \(\vv\text{.}\)

(f) True/False.

Given any two non-zero vectors \(\vu\) and \(\vv\) in \(\R^2\text{,}\) we can obtain any vector in \(\R^2\) as a linear combination of \(\vu\) and \(\vv\text{.}\)

(g) True/False.

Given any two distinct vectors \(\vu\) and \(\vv\) in \(\R^2\text{,}\) we can obtain any vector in \(\R^2\) as a linear combination of \(\vu\) and \(\vv\text{.}\)

(h) True/False.

If \(\vu\) can be expressed as a linear combination of \(\vv_1\) and \(\vv_2\text{,}\) then \(2\vu\) can also be expressed as a linear combination of \(\vv_1\) and \(\vv_2\text{.}\)

(i) True/False.

The span of any two vectors neither of which is a multiple of the other can be visualized as a plane through the origin.

(j) True/False.

Given any vector, the collection of all linear combinations of this vector can be visualized as a line through the origin.

(k) True/False.

The span of any collection of vectors includes the \(\vzero\) vector.

(l) True/False.

If the span of \(\vv_1\) and \(\vv_2\) is all of \(\R^2\text{,}\) then so is the span of \(\vv_1\) and \(\vv_1+\vv_2\text{.}\)

(m) True/False.

If the span of \(\vv_1, \vv_2\) and \(\vv_3\) is all of \(\R^3\text{,}\) then so is the span of \(\vv_1+\vv_2\) and \(\vv_2+\vv_3\text{.}\)
