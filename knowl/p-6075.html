Recall that an inner product on \(\R^n\) assigns to each pair of vectors \(\vu\) and \(\vv\) the scalar \(\langle \vu, \vv \rangle\text{.}\) Thus, an inner product on \(\R^n\) defines a mapping from \(\R^n \times \R^n\) to \(\R\text{.}\) Recall also that an inner product on \(\R^n\) is commutative, distributes over vector addition, and respects scalar multiplication, and the inner product of a vector in \(\R^n\) by itself is always non-negative and is equal to 0 only when the vector is the zero vector.
