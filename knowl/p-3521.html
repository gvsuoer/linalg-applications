Recall that a rotation matrix is of the form

\begin{equation*} R_\theta= \left[ \begin{array}{cr} \cos(\theta)\amp -\sin(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta)\amp \cos(\theta) \end{array} \right] \end{equation*}

where the rotation is counterclockwise about the origin by an angle of \(\theta\) radians. In ActivityÂ 21.2, we considered the rotation matrix with angle \(\pi/2\) in counterclockwise direction. We will soon see that rotation matrices play an important role in the geometry of a matrix transformation for a matrix that has complex eigenvalues. In this activity, we will restrict ourselves to the \(2 \times 2\) case, but similar arguments can be made in higher dimensions.
