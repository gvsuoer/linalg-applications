As you noticed in Preview ActivityÂ 21.1, the complex roots of the characteristic equation of a real matrix \(A\) come in complex conjugate pairs. This should come as no surprise since we know through our use of the quadratic formula that complex roots of (real) quadratic polynomials come in complex conjugate pairs. More generally, if \(p(x)=a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2 + \cdots + a_nx^n\) is a polynomial with real coefficients and \(z\) is a root of this polynomial, meaning \(p(z)=0\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} 0=\overline{p(z)} = \overline{a_0+a_1z+a_2z^2 + \cdots + a_nz^n} = a_0 + a_1 \overline{z} + a_2 \overline{z}^2 + \cdots + a_n \overline{z}^n = p(\overline{z})\,\text{.} \end{equation*}

Therefore, \(\overline{z}\) is also a root of \(p(x)\text{.}\)
