(a)

Given vectors

\begin{equation*} \vv =\left[ \begin{array}{r} 1 \\ -2 \\ 2 \end{array} \right] \, , \, \vu=\left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1\\ 3 \end{array} \right] \, , \, \vw= \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1\\ 1\\ 4 \end{array} \right]\,\text{,} \end{equation*}

determine the components of the vector \(3\vv + \vu - 2\vw\) using the operations defined above.

(b)

In mathematics, any time we define operations on objects, such as addition of vectors, we ask which properties the operation has. For example, one might wonder if \(\vu+\vv=\vv+\vu\) for any two vectors \(\vu, \vv\) of the same size. If this property holds, we say that the addition of vectors is a commutative operation. However, to verify this property we cannot use examples since the property must hold for any two vectors. For simplicity, we focus on two-dimensional vectors \(\vu = \left[ \begin{array}{c} u_1\\ u_2 \end{array} \right]\) and \(\vv = \left[ \begin{array}{c} v_1\\ v_2 \end{array} \right]\text{.}\) Using these arbitrary vectors, can we say that \(\vu+\vv=\vv+\vu\text{?}\) If so, justify. If not, give a counterexample. (Note: Giving a counterexample is the best way to justify why a general statement is not true.)

(c)

One way to geometrically represent vectors with two components uses a point in the plane to correspond to a vector. Specifically, the vector \(\left[ \begin{array}{c} x \\ y \end{array} \right]\) corresponds to the point \((x, y)\) in the plane. As a specific example, the vector \(\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \end{array} \right]\) corresponds to the point \((1, 2)\) in the plane. This representation will be especially handy when we consider infinite collections of vectors as we will do in this problem.

(i)

On the same set of axes, plot the points that correspond to 5-6 scalar multiples of the vector \(\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \end{array} \right]\text{.}\) Make sure to use variety of scalar multiples covering possibilities with \(c>0, c\lt 0, c>1, 0\lt c\lt 1, -1\lt c\lt 0\text{.}\) If we consider the collection of all possible scalar multiples of this vector, what do we obtain?

(ii)

What would the collection of all scalar multiples of the vector \(\left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \end{array} \right]\) form in the plane?

(iii)

What would the collection of all scalar multiples of the vector \(\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1\\ 1 \end{array} \right]\) form in the three-dimensional space?

(d)

Let \(\vu=\left[ \begin{array}{c} 1\\ 2 \end{array} \right]\) and \(\vv=\left[ \begin{array}{r} 1\\ -1 \end{array} \right]\) in \(\R^2\text{.}\) We are interested in finding all vectors that can be formed as a sum of scalar multiples of \(\vu\) and \(\vv\text{.}\)

(i)

On the same set of axes, plot the points that correspond to the vectors \(\vu, \vv, \vu+\vv, 1.5\vu, 2\vv, -\vu, -\vv, -\vu+2\vv\text{.}\) Plot other random sums of scalar multiples of \(\vu\) and \(\vv\) using several scalar multiples (including those less than 1 or negative) (that is, find other vectors of the form \(a\vu + b\vv\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are any scalars.).

(ii)

If we considered sums of all scalar multiples of \(\vu, \vv\text{,}\) which vectors will we obtain? Can we obtain any vector in \(\R^2\) in this form?
