In general, a matrix derived from a table like TableÂ 5.7 is called a consumption matrix, which we will denote as \(C\text{.}\) (In the example discussed here \(C = \left[\begin{array}{ccc} 0.25 \amp 0.28 \amp 0.27 \\ 0.20 \amp 0.60 \amp 0.60 \\ 0.55 \amp 0.12 \amp 0.13 \end{array} \right]\text{.}\)) A consumption matrix \(C = [c_{ij}]\text{,}\) where \(c_{ij}\) represents the proportion of the output of sector \(j\) that is consumed by sector \(i\text{,}\) satisfies two important properties.

  • Since no sector can consume a negative amount or an amount that exceeds the output of another sector, we must have \(0 \leq c_{ij} \leq 1\) for all \(i\) and \(j\text{.}\)

  • If there are \(n\) sectors in the economy, the fact that all output is consumed within the economy implies that \(c_{1j}+c_{2j}+ \cdots + c_{nj} = 1\text{.}\) In other words, the column sums of \(C\) are all 1.
